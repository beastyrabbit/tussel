Euclidean rhythms use the `(k,n)` notation to distribute `k` hits across `n` steps as evenly as possible. Many traditional rhythms from around the world are Euclidean.

## Code

<CodeComparison>
  <Fragment slot="tussel">
```ts
setcps(0.5);
stack(
  s("bd(3,8)").gain(0.9),
  s("hh(5,8)").gain(0.4),
  s("cp(2,5)").gain(0.6).pan(-0.5)
)
```

## What's Happening

- `"bd(3,8)"` distributes 3 kicks across 8 steps → `x . . x . . x .` (Cuban tresillo)
- `"hh(5,8)"` distributes 5 hi-hats across 8 steps → `x . x x . x x .` (a common off-beat pattern)
- `"cp(2,5)"` distributes 2 claps across 5 steps → `x . x . .`
- `.pan(-0.5)` moves the claps slightly left in the stereo field
- The three patterns have different cycle lengths (8, 8, 5), creating a **polyrhythmic** feel as they phase against each other
- Euclidean notation `(k,n)` is a mini-notation feature shared by Tidal, Strudel, and Tussel
